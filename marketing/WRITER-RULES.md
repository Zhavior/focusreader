# Hyperfi Writer's Rulebook
## How to write for ADHD readers — psychology-grounded rules for every piece of content

> Give this document to anyone who writes a blog post, landing page, email, or
> TikTok caption for Hyperfi. These rules are not style preferences. Each one
> exists because of how ADHD attention, motivation, and emotion actually work.
> Break a rule only when you can name the rule and why you're breaking it.

---

## Part 1 — The reader you are writing for

Before the rules, understand the brain. ADHD is not a knowledge deficit or a
discipline deficit. It is a **regulation** difference: of attention, of effort,
of emotion, of time perception. Your reader:

- Has been told "just try harder" their whole life and is **exquisitely
  sensitive to shame** (many experience rejection-sensitive dysphoria — criticism
  lands ~10x harder than you intend).
- Decides in **1–3 seconds** whether your piece is worth their attention, and
  re-decides every paragraph.
- Has likely already tried five tools and abandoned them. Skepticism is the
  default; hype pattern-matches to disappointment.
- Is often *very* smart and *very* online. They know what dopamine is. They can
  smell pop-neuroscience nonsense and they will roast it publicly.

Everything below follows from this.

## Part 2 — The Ten Rules

### Rule 1: Validate before you solve. (Never shame.)
The ADHD reader's first question is not "what's the solution?" — it's "does this
person get it?" Open by describing their experience accurately, in the second
person, without judgment. Words like *lazy, excuses, discipline, just, simply,
should* are banned unless you are explicitly dismantling them.

- ❌ "Struggling to focus? Stop making excuses."
- ✅ "You've read the same paragraph four times. It's not laziness — it's how
  interest-based attention works."

**Psychology:** shame triggers avoidance, not action, in brains with RSD.
Validation earns the attention you need for everything after.

### Rule 2: The first two lines carry the whole piece.
Deliver the payoff (or the recognition moment) in the first two sentences.
No wind-ups, no "in today's fast-paced world." Every post gets a **TL;DR box at
the top** — readers who only read that should still get value.

**Psychology:** ADHD attention is interest-gated. There is no "they'll get to
the good part." There is only the good part, first, or nothing.

### Rule 3: Put a time estimate on everything.
Every article: "4 min read (or 2 min at the TL;DRs)." Every step in a how-to:
how long it takes. This is also our product's signature — the content should
*practice* the time math we sell.

**Psychology:** time blindness makes unbounded tasks feel infinite, and
infinite tasks don't get started. A bounded task ("4 minutes") feels finishable
— and *finishable* is our entire brand.

### Rule 4: Write in chunks a drifting eye can re-enter.
- Paragraphs: **3 sentences max.**
- A subheading every 150–200 words — and subheadings must carry the argument by
  themselves (a skimmer reading only headers gets the full story).
- **Bold the key phrase** in important paragraphs (one per paragraph, max) so a
  skimming eye snags on it.
- Bullets over prose whenever order doesn't matter.

**Psychology:** attention *will* drift mid-article; that's not failure, it's
physiology. Chunked structure gives the returning eye a re-entry point instead
of a wall of text that demands starting over — the exact experience that made
them abandon reading in the first place.

### Rule 5: Concrete beats abstract, always.
Numbers, scenarios, named examples. "A 47-minute chapter becomes 26 minutes at
1.8×" — not "save significant time." "It's 11pm and the reading is due at 9am"
— not "when deadlines loom."

**Psychology:** ADHD working memory is taxed by abstraction — it forces the
reader to hold and translate. A concrete scene is *seen*, not computed, and
specificity is also the strongest credibility signal to a skeptical audience.

### Rule 6: One idea per piece. One CTA per piece.
Each post argues exactly one thing. End with exactly one action ("try the free
5,000 characters"), not a menu of links.

**Psychology:** choice overload is a decision-paralysis machine for executive
dysfunction. Every added option measurably reduces action. Cut your darlings or
they'll cost you the conversion.

### Rule 7: Use science honestly or not at all.
You may reference: interest-based attention, working memory load, time
blindness, dual-coding (audio + visual), the Zeigarnik effect, body doubling.
You may NOT: over-claim ("this hacks your dopamine!"), cite studies you haven't
read, or present our features as clinical treatments. Say "designed around,"
never "proven to treat." Hyperfi is a study tool, not a medical device — legally
and ethically.

**Psychology (and law):** this audience includes people who read the actual
papers. One caught exaggeration costs all trust. FTC rules on health claims
cost more than that.

### Rule 8: Be honest about competitors — by name.
When a competitor is better at something, say so ("ElevenLabs Reader is free
and the voices are stunning — if all you want is a voice, use it"). Then draw
the real line: a voice is not a system for finishing.

**Psychology:** conceding a point is the single fastest trust-builder with a
burned-before audience. Readers who feel you're being fair with rivals believe
what you say about yourself.

### Rule 9: Never romanticize, never catastrophize.
No "ADHD is a superpower!" (it dismisses real struggle). No tragedy framing
either (they live it; they don't need it narrated). The register is: *matter-of-
fact, wry, on their side.* Humor is welcome — self-aware, never at the reader's
expense. Person-first vs. identity-first language: mirror the community — most
online ADHD adults say "ADHDer" or "I'm ADHD"; don't correct them.

**Psychology:** both extremes read as outsiders performing. The community
rewards writers who sound like *members* — because the writer either is one or
listened hard enough to sound like one.

### Rule 10: End with the finish line visible.
Close every piece by shrinking the next step to something under two minutes:
"Paste one page into Hyperfi. That's it. That's the whole assignment."

**Psychology:** task initiation is the #1 barrier. The Zeigarnik effect means a
*started* task nags to be finished — so our job in every closing paragraph is
to engineer the smallest possible start.

## Part 3 — Voice & mechanics cheat sheet

| | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| **Tone** | A sharp friend who also has ADHD | A clinician, a coach, a hype-man |
| **Person** | "You" and "we" | "ADHD sufferers," "these people," "patients" |
| **Verbs** | Finish, start, hear, click | Optimize, unlock, revolutionize, supercharge |
| **Evidence** | "One study found…" + a real link | "Studies show…" + nothing |
| **Emoji** | Occasional, load-bearing (✅ 26 min) | Confetti |
| **Sentence rhythm** | Vary hard. Short punch. Then a longer one that carries the idea forward before landing. | Uniform medium-length sentences (they blur) |

**Formatting floor for every blog post:** TL;DR box → time estimate → hook that
validates → chunked body with argument-carrying subheads → one concrete product
moment (shown, not pitched) → single CTA → no exit pop-ups (we do not ambush
ADHD readers, ever).

**The final test before publishing — read it and ask:**
1. Could a skimmer reading only the bold phrases and headers retell the argument?
2. Does anything in it shame the reader, even gently?
3. Is there exactly one CTA?
4. Would an ADHD adult who has tried and abandoned six apps roll their eyes anywhere?

If any answer is wrong, it isn't done.
